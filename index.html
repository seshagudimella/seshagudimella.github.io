Three stories that define how I approach platform engineering — with urgency, precision, and long-term thinking.

01 — Cloud Cost Optimization
From Consistently Over Budget to $100K Saved Every Month
$100K
/ month saved

Our AWS spend was running $80–110K over budget every month — peaking at $782K against a $675K target. Rather than treating this as a finance problem, I treated it as an engineering one.

I led a systematic cost reduction program: rightsizing compute instances to match actual workload profiles, migrating eligible jobs to Spot Fleet, replacing on-demand spend with Reserved Instances and Savings Plans, and aggressively eliminating idle and orphaned resources across accounts.

The outcome: total spend fell from a $782K peak to $665K — the first month we came in under our $675K budget. Savings are now structural, backed by real-time guardrails that keep every team accountable to their cloud footprint.

02 — CI Pipeline Observability
50% CI Cost Cut — and Engineers Got Dramatically Faster Too
~50%
GKE cost reduction

The engineering org runs ~2,000 Jenkins jobs per day across a complex build, test, and deploy cycle. Debugging failures meant context-switching between multiple platforms. Worse, CI pipelines ran overnight US time — meaning the EU team waited nearly a full day for feedback on every commit.

I implemented Datadog CI Pipeline Visibility to create a unified view across all Jenkins jobs — build, test, and deploy — replacing manual monthly spreadsheets with daily SLOs on execution time, queue time, and blocked time.

The result: ~50% reduction in GKE CI costs through workload optimization, and CI feedback collapsed from ~1 day to near real-time for both US and EU teams — without duplicating infrastructure. Datadog featured this work in their official case study.

03 — Platform Strategy
One Ecosystem: From Fragmented Cost Tools to a Single Source of Truth
3 tools
→ 1

Cost visibility was scattered across the org: AWS Cost Explorer for raw billing data, nOps for cost reporting, and separate systems for performance and infrastructure monitoring. Teams were constantly context-switching, and cost data lived in a silo disconnected from the engineering decisions that drove it.

I led the strategic migration of cost observability into Datadog — consolidating cost reports, CI metrics, infrastructure performance, and alerting into a single organizational data ecosystem. By putting cost data next to the performance metrics that cause it, teams could no longer ignore the relationship between the two.

Today, engineering teams across the org operate from real-time cost and performance dashboards in a single platform — making cost a first-class engineering concern, not a quarterly finance review.

Principles

How I think about platform engineering

The beliefs that guide every architectural decision, team conversation, and tooling choice.

01
Observability is a culture, not a tool.
You can deploy the best monitoring stack in the world and still fly blind — if teams don't own their signals. Real observability is built through habits, accountability, and dashboards engineers actually look at.
02
Cost is an engineering problem.
Cloud spend doesn't belong in a finance spreadsheet reviewed once a quarter. It belongs next to your performance metrics, visible to every engineer, every day. When cost and code share a dashboard, behavior changes.
03
Proactive guardrails beat reactive firefighting.
The goal isn't to be fast at fixing incidents — it's to make incidents rarer. Every alert threshold, every budget guardrail, every SLO established is time bought back for the engineering team to build instead of debug.
04
Consolidate before you complicate.
Tool sprawl is a stealth tax on engineering velocity. Before adopting something new, I ask: what can we eliminate? The most impactful infrastructure upgrade I've made wasn't adding a platform — it was collapsing three tools into one.
05
Shift left on everything.
Performance, cost, reliability — these shouldn't be discovered in production. The platform team's job is to move feedback as close to the code commit as possible, so engineers get the right signal at the moment they can still act on it.
